  • Yongcai Wang is currently associate professor at the Lab of Intelligent Network, department of Computer Sciences, Renmin University of China. His current research interests include network localization, simultaneously locating and mapping (SLAM), combinatorial optimization and applications. He received his BS and PhD degrees from department of automation sciences and engineering, Tsinghua University in 2001 and 2006 respectively. He worked as associated researcher at NEC Labs. China from 2007-2009. He was an research scientist in Institute for Interdisciplinary Information Sciences, Tsinghua University from 2009-2015. He was visiting scholar at Department of Electronic and Computer Engineering of Cornell University in 2015. He has more than 10 patients and has publised more than 50 papers in top-tier conferences and journals. He was demo session chair of CWSN2011, commitee member of CWSN2013, IThings2013. He is member of IEEE, China Computer Federation (CCF) and Chinese Association of Automation (CAA). He is member of the professional committee of DEDS of CAA.

Journal Paper

1. Yongcai Wang, Tianyuan Sun, Guoyao Rao, Deying Li, Formation Tracking in Sparse Airborne Networks, IEEE Journal on Selected Areas in Communications (JSAC) 2018, IF 7.17 (CCF A)
This paper studies how to track the 3D topology of UAV network by partially measured inter-distances among the UAVs . It considers the case when the measured inter-UAV distances are sparse and noisy, and the topology of UAV network is changing continuously. This paper exploits the fact that the well-connected subcomponents exist widely and their local structures can be calculated reliably, because the unevenness of node distribution in sparse networks. Therefore, a 3D weighted component stitching (WCS) method to find the reliable components and stitch their local structures with weights is proposed for calculating the formation of the network accurately. It includes a (2-4-star) detection and merging algorithms to extract the reliable global rigid components and a weighted component-based Kalman filter (WCKF) algorithm. Experiments show 21%–48% formation tracking accuracy improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods.
2. Tianyuan Sun, Yongcai Wang, et al., WCS: Weighted Component Stitching for Sparse Network Localization, ACM/IEEE Transactions on Networking (TON), 2018, Volume: 26 , Issue: 5 , Oct. 2018. IF 3.11 （CCF A）
This paper studies the network localization problem in sparse networks , which are highly challenging because the graph is generally not global rigid. This paper leverages the facts that there are well-connected subgraphs which can be realized rather confidentially. So it proposes a Weighted Component Stitching (WCS) method to find redundantly rigid components with high redundant ratios to generate reliable local realization. Then the localized components are weightly stitched to produce the global network structure. It proves that finding and ranking the redundantly rigid components is an NP-hard problem. A series of theorems and algorithms to carry out WCS efficiently are therefore proposed. The simulation results show that the localization accuracy can be improved (45%–64%) by WCS over the state-of-the-art algorithms under various settings of sparse networks with different noises.
3. Buke Ao, Yongcai Wang, Hongnan Liu, Deying Li, Lei Song, Jianqiang Li, Context Impacts in Accelerometer-Based Walk Detection and Step Counting. Sensors 18(11): 3604 (2018) (IF 2.8)
Various algorithms have been proposed for Walk Detection (WD) and Step Counting (SC), but these algorithms are generally sensitive to the contexts of applications, i.e., (1) the locations of sensor placement; (2) the sensor orientations; (3) the user’s walking patterns; (4) the preprocessing window sizes; and (5) the sensor sampling rates. A thorough understanding of how these dynamic factors affect the algorithms’ performances is investigated and compared in this paper. In particular, representative WD and SC algorithms are introduced according to their design methodologies. A series of experiments is designed in consideration of different application contexts to form an experimental dataset. Different algorithms are then implemented and evaluated on the dataset. The evaluation results provide a quantitative performance comparison indicating the advantages and weaknesses of different algorithms under different application scenarios, giving valuable guidance for algorithm selection in practical applications.
